Guide

The grid drawing method, made easy

The grid drawing method is the classic way to copy or resize a picture by hand without tracing: divide your reference into a grid of equal squares, draw the same grid on your paper or canvas, then redraw each square one at a time. GridGen does the fiddly part — it overlays a precise, labeled grid on your reference photo and exports a print-ready PDF in seconds.

What the grid method is

Artists have used the grid method for centuries to transfer proportions accurately. The idea is simple: a complex image is hard to draw all at once, but a single small square — just a few lines and shapes — is easy. By copying the reference square by square, you keep every proportion correct and let the whole picture come together on its own.

You need two matching grids: one over the reference image and one on your drawing surface. GridGen produces the first; you draw the second lightly in pencil. Labeling both grids the same way — letters across the top, numbers down the side — means square B3 on the reference always maps to square B3 on your paper.

It is a visual aid, nothing more. GridGen overlays an even grid of labeled squares so you can copy by eye. It does not trace, redraw, or alter your image — the artwork is still all yours.

Step by step with GridGen

  1. Upload your reference. Drag a photo or drawing into GridGen, or click to select one. It never leaves your browser.
  2. Choose By cells. Pick the number of columns and rows — a 10 × 10 grid is a common starting point. Fewer, larger squares are easier for beginners; more, smaller squares give finer control over detail.
  3. Add chess (A1) labels, outside. Letters and numbers in the margins keep every square named without drawing over the picture.
  4. Lower the opacity. A thin line at reduced opacity sits lightly over the reference so it stays easy to read.
  5. Export the PDF and draw your grid. Print the reference, then pencil the same number of squares onto your paper or canvas and copy square by square.

The full control reference lives in the user guide; for charts where one square equals one stitch or bead, see cross-stitch & counted charts.

Scaling up or down

The grid method is also the simplest way to change size while keeping proportions exact. The two grids only have to share the same number of squares, not the same square size:

  • Enlarge — a 10 × 10 grid on a small reference maps square-for-square onto a 10 × 10 grid of bigger squares on a larger sheet, wall, or canvas. Muralists and sign painters scale this way.
  • Reduce — the same trick in reverse shrinks a large reference onto a smaller surface.

Because every square holds the same fraction of the picture at either size, the proportions transfer perfectly.

Tips for a clean transfer

  • Match the proportions. Use the same width-to-height ratio of squares as your paper, so the picture fills the surface without distortion.
  • Draw your grid lightly. A faint pencil grid erases cleanly once the drawing is done.
  • Work one square at a time. Look only at the square you are on; copy the lines and shapes inside it, then move on.
  • Start with the busy squares. Tackle eyes, hands, or other landmarks first to anchor the rest.
  • Use more squares for more detail. If a square holds too much, raise the cell count and reprint.

When your grid is set, open the app and export — the live preview updates as you adjust.

FAQ

What is the grid drawing method?

It is a technique for copying or resizing an image by hand: you overlay a grid of equal squares on a reference, draw a matching grid on your paper, and redraw the picture one square at a time so the proportions stay accurate.

How many squares should I use?

Start around 8×8 to 10×10 for a simple subject, and use more, smaller squares for fine detail. With GridGen you can reprint at a higher cell count any time a square holds too much to copy comfortably.

Does GridGen draw the picture for me?

No. GridGen only overlays the labeled grid and exports a PDF. You still draw the picture by hand — it is a guide for your own work, not a tracer or an image generator.

Can I use the grid method to enlarge a drawing?

Yes. Give your reference and your surface the same number of squares but make the squares bigger on the larger surface. Each square holds the same part of the picture, so the proportions scale exactly.

Do I need an account, and is my image private?

No account is needed and it is free. Your image is processed entirely in your browser and never uploaded, so your reference stays private.
